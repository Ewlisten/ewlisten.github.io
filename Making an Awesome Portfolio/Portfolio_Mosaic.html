Where does he score from?

The shot chart doesn't just show makes and misses. It shows the geometry of a player's game — the zones they dominate, the spots they avoid, the corners where they're quietly more efficient than anyone mentions. With Wembanyama, the shape is unlike any player the league has seen: rim finishes, corner threes, and pull-up threes from step-back range coexist in a single season profile. Every dot is a decision. The pattern is the player.

Wembanyama's post-All-Star shot profile shifts measurably — more above-break threes, fewer mid-range attempts. The chart makes that evolution visible without a single word of commentary.

Who does the data say he's becoming?

The ML model maps every player in the league as a vector across five dimensions, then surfaces who they most resemble using cosine similarity. When the comparisons return unexpected names — a sophomore who moves statistically like a veteran All-Star — you've found something the box score obscured.

The radar makes the gap visual. The orange shape is the subject. The ghost shape is their nearest comparable. The space between them is the question the model is answering.

A 12% cosine distance can mean everything — or nothing. The chart forces you to decide which stats actually drive the comparison.

Where on the track does the gap actually open up?

A two-second gap at the flag looks like one thing in the standings and a completely different thing in the data. The Lap Time Analyzer pulls real race telemetry via FastF1 and breaks every lap into sectors — showing not just who was faster, but at which corner, on which lap, under which tire compound the delta grew.

Sector 2 deltas often reveal more than overall lap time. It's where setup decisions, brake bias, and driver commitment intersect — the invisible part of the race.

Same car. What does the data reveal?

The teammate comparison is the cleanest experiment in motorsport — same machinery, same strategy, different driver. The Driver vs. Teammate Tool neutralizes the car advantage and isolates individual pace across qualifying and race stints.

When one driver is consistently 0.3s faster through the high-speed complex and 0.1s slower under braking, that's not noise. That's a skill profile.

Controlling for car performance is the key move. Without it, you're comparing driver talent plus machinery — which tells you nothing about either.

Can a model trace the exact arc of a Wembanyama step-back?

This pipeline applies computer vision to one of the most unguardable shots in the NBA — Wembanyama's step-back jumper. Frame by frame, it extracts ball position, calculates trajectory, and annotates the flight path with release angle and arc data. The output makes visible what broadcast angles obscure: the precise geometry that makes this shot so hard to contest.

Trajectory modeling at the player level turns broadcast footage into measurement data — the same kind that coaching staffs pay millions to collect manually.

Tracking Tyrese Maxey — one of the fastest players alive

Applied YOLO detection and SORT tracking to Tyrese Maxey footage, locking a persistent bounding box onto him through contact, screens, and full-speed cuts. At Maxey's pace, maintaining ID integrity across frames is a genuine technical challenge — one that pure detection models fail under pressure.

The annotated output logs position and proximity to other tracked players on every frame — the raw material for automated spacing and off-ball movement analysis that no box score captures.

When the tracker holds lock at 40+ mph cutting speed without re-assigning IDs, the model is generalizing — not memorizing camera angles.
